Research

My work sits at the intersection of engineering, economics, and criminology. I study how cybercriminal ecosystems evolve — the marketplaces and forums where access, tools, and stolen data change hands, the trust signals that let strangers do business safely, and the migration patterns that show where the ecosystem is heading next. Increasingly, that means asking how AI changes both sides of the equation: how it's used to attack, and how it can be used to understand and infiltrate these communities for threat intelligence.

Publications & Working Papers

Research outputs so far, newest first, linked where a public version exists. For the complete, continuously updated record see my Google Scholar profile.

  • DMITRY: A Multi-Agent Framework for Active Cyber Threat Intelligence Elicitation

    In preparation — expected 2027

    Structured extraction and safety-control methods for gathering interaction-dependent threat intelligence — vendor pricing, capabilities, delivery workflows — without compromising the collector.

  • TeleHunt: A Framework and Tool for Efficient Cybercriminal Community Discovery on Telegram

    Roy Ricaldi, Victor Asanache, Luca Allodi — IWCC, 2026

    An LLM-assisted framework for finding and evaluating cybercriminal communities on Telegram at scale, built around Telegram's own invite-link economy.

  • Topical Shifts in the Dark Web: A Longitudinal Analysis of Content from the Cybercrime Ecosystem

    Roy Ricaldi, Maximilian Schäfer, Philipp Zech, Luca Allodi, Raffaela Groner, Irdin Pekaric — IEEE EuroS&PW, 2026

    Large-scale topic modelling across dark web forums and marketplaces shows the ecosystem evolves mainly by shifting emphasis within stable themes, not by constantly producing new ones.

  • Uncovering the Trust Signals Supporting Telegram's Cybercrime Economy

    Roy Ricaldi, Tina Marjanov, Luca Allodi, Alice Hutchings — eCrime 2025

    Maps the trust signals — escrow, vouching, automation — that cybercriminals rely on, helping analysts prioritise where to focus collection efforts.

  • An Experimental Design to Investigate Attacker Actions on an Access-as-a-Service 'Criminal' Platform

    Roy Ricaldi, Yasen Yalamov, Michele Campobasso, Luca Allodi, Hannah Kool, Asier Moneva, Eric Rutger Leukfeldt — IEEE EuroS&PW 2025

    A simulated access-as-a-service marketplace used to study how attackers choose targets, and what that reveals about their sophistication.

  • Framing 'migratory' decisions in the criminal underground

    Roy Ricaldi, Luca Allodi, Jai Wientjes, Adriana Radu — arXiv preprint, 2024

    Uses the economics of migration to explain the shift from forums to Telegram, comparing pricing, products, and behaviour across both.

Teaching & Supervision

I'm a teaching assistant on two TU Eindhoven courses:

  • Offensive Computer Security (2IC80)2024 – 2025
  • Information Security Technology Seminar (2IMS00)2024 – 2028

Within the IST seminar, I supervise student projects on cybercrime and threat intelligence topics. If you have your own project idea, reach out — or ask what topics I currently have open. Taking this seminar with me is also a natural first step toward a thesis: it's a lower-stakes way for us to find out whether our interests and working styles line up, before committing to something longer.

Beyond the seminar, I also supervise master's students working on cybercrime and threat intelligence topics directly, combining technical depth with an eye for what's realistically researchable within a thesis timeline. I am a very hands-on supervisor. I am committed to providing weekly supervision and working collaboratively with all my students.

Students I've Supervised
Jai Wientjes Role of Telegram in Cybercrime Forums — Jai investigated how underground forum users employ Telegram as a tool to facilitate not only private communication but also support their operational pipeline, with some Telegram environments serving as marketplaces, Telegram bots providing automated services, and more purpose roles.]
Mels van Loosen Comparing Offerings on Telegram and Underground Forums — Mels compared Telegram-based cybercrime marketplaces with traditional dark web forums, examining the products they offer, their pricing, and the trust mechanisms they use to facilitate illegal trade.
Yasen Yalamov A System for Active Data Collection of Access-as-a-Service Users — Yasen engineered a system for active data collection of users interested in Access-as-a-Service offerings, designing and deploying multiple layers for the experimental setup, collecting telemetry throughout.
Victor Asanache A Tool for Efficient Cybercriminal Content Discovery on Telegram — Victor implemented a tool that can discover pointers to cybercrime groups and channels in Telegram by leveraging NLP models for classification into distinct market segments.
David Klaassen Barba Marketplace Preferences and In-Bot Behaviors of Access-as-a-Service Users — David worked on analyzing how users looking to buy access to bots choose their targets and what they do once they gain access to them.
Bianca Eni Mapping Cybercrime Communities on Telegram — Bianca mapped and analyzed Telegram cybercrime communities by combining network analysis, AI-based topic labeling, and threat intelligence extraction to reveal hidden relationships and support cybercrime investigations.
Kris Kyurkchiev LLM Agents for Active Cyber Threat Intelligence Collection — Kris and I are developing a framework that engages with cybercriminal vendors on Telegram to safely collect structured cyber threat intelligence through conversation.
Rodrigo Martin Nuñez Purpose of Underground Forum Threads and Their External Communication References — Rodrigo and I are developing a framework that classifies underground forum threads and their external communication references (e.g., Telegram, Discord) to improve filtering and prioritization.
